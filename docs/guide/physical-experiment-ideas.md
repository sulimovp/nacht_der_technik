# On-site physical experiment — light, duality & "particles behave differently"

> A small, cheap, kid-safe optical demo to run **live at the table** next to the digital games. It
> gives visitors something *real* to look at: light behaving as a **wave**, the idea of the **photon**
> as a particle, and an honest analog of **quantum tunnelling**. Ties straight into the games'
> superposition/measurement story. EN with short DE staff lines.

## Why do this
The digital games teach quantum *logic* (superposition, measurement, randomness). A physical optic
demo shows the *phenomenon* underneath: that light is both a wave and a stream of particles, and that
at small scales things do "impossible" things. It's the perfect 60-second wow to hook a family.

Important honesty up front: an **iPhone flashlight is incoherent white light**. It's great for
**diffraction/spectrum** demos (wave nature), but for crisp **double-slit interference** you want a
cheap **laser pointer**. Both are listed below; pick based on budget and safety comfort.

---

## Experiment A — Wave nature: diffraction & spectrum (iPhone flashlight) ✅ easiest, safest
**Shows:** light is a **wave** — it spreads and splits into colours.
**Materials (a few francs):** iPhone flashlight; a **diffraction grating slide/film** (or a **CD/DVD**,
or a cheap cardboard **hand spectroscope**); optionally a narrow slit made from two razor cuts in foil.
**Do it:**
- Shine the flashlight through the grating / reflect off a CD → a **rainbow** fans out.
- Or look at the flashlight through the grating held to the eye → coloured copies of the light.
- Squeeze light through a very thin slit (or between two fingers almost touching) → soft **fringes**.
**Say (kid):** "Light looks white, but it's really made of many colours — and it spreads out in stripes.
That striping only happens for **waves**." / DE: "Licht ist eine Welle — darum fächert es sich in Farben und Streifen auf."
**Ties to games:** waves → the "possibility waves" of superposition; colours → photons of different energy.

---

## Experiment B — Wave–particle duality: the double slit (laser pointer) ⭐ the centrepiece
**Shows:** the famous **duality** — single particles of light build up a **wave interference** pattern.
**Materials:** a **≤1 mW class-1/2 green or red laser pointer** (a few francs); a **double-slit slide**
(buy one, or make one: blacken a glass slide with candle soot and draw two fine parallel scratches; or
two close pencil-lines on exposed film). A dark wall ~2–3 m away.
**Do it:** shine the laser through the double slit onto the wall → a row of **bright/dark bands**
(interference). Only waves interfere — yet light is also photons.
**Say (teen):** "Fire the laser through two slits and you get stripes — interference, a wave thing. But
if you dimmed it enough to send **one photon at a time**, the stripes still build up dot by dot. Each
photon somehow goes through **both** slits and interferes with itself. That's wave–particle duality."
/ DE: "Ein einzelnes Lichtteilchen geht durch beide Spalte zugleich und überlagert sich selbst — Welle und Teilchen in einem."
**Support:** loop the classic single-photon double-slit build-up video on the screen (search
"single photon double slit build up") so kids *see* the dots forming fringes.
**Ties to games:** "both paths at once" = **superposition** (the Quantum-Penalty two-corner shot!),
"look which slit → stripes vanish" = **measurement/collapse**.

> ⚠️ **Laser safety:** staff-operated only; ≤1 mW; **never** point at anyone's eyes or reflective
> surfaces at eye level; aim down at the slit/wall. Consider a fixed clamp so kids don't grab it.

---

## Experiment C — "Particles do the impossible": quantum-tunnelling analog (FTIR) 🔦
**Shows:** an honest optical analog of **quantum tunnelling** — light crosses a gap it "shouldn't".
**The physics:** in **total internal reflection**, light inside glass/water bounces back completely.
But if a second surface is brought **within a wavelength** of the first, light **leaks (tunnels)**
across the tiny gap — "frustrated total internal reflection" (FTIR). It's the *same evanescent-wave
maths* as a particle tunnelling through a barrier.
**Materials:** two glass **prisms** (best), or the kid-proof version: a **clear glass of water** + your
finger; or a clear plastic bag pressed on glass.
**Do it (simple):** look through the side of a water glass at a shallow angle — the far wall looks like
a **mirror** (total internal reflection). Now press a wet **fingertip** on the outside: where the ridges
touch, light **tunnels** across and you see the **fingerprint glowing** through the "mirror."
**Say (kid):** "The glass acts like a perfect mirror — until your finger gets *super* close. Then light
sneaks across a gap it shouldn't cross. Tiny quantum particles do exactly this: they slip through walls
that should stop them. It's called **tunnelling** — and it's how the sun shines and how some computer
chips work." / DE: "Winzige Teilchen schlüpfen durch Wände, die sie eigentlich stoppen sollten — das nennt man **Tunneln**."
**Ties to games:** reinforces "the small world plays by different rules."

## Recommended minimal kit (cheapest strong version)
- iPhone flashlight (have it) + **1 diffraction grating film** (Exp. A).
- **1 low-power laser pointer + 1 double-slit slide** (Exp. B — the star). ~CHF 10–20 total.
- A **water glass** for the tunnelling analog (Exp. C — zero cost).
- The single-photon double-slit **video** looping on the stand screen.

## How it fits the stand story (say this)
"Out here you see it's **real**: light is a wave (rainbow), yet made of particles (photons), and it does
impossible things (tunnelling). On the screens you then *play* with those same rules — a ball in two
corners at once, randomness you can't cheat. Same physics, once as a demo, once as a game."

## Fallback if no laser
Do **A** (diffraction) + **C** (tunnelling with a water glass) + the double-slit **video**. You still
land wave nature, the photon, tunnelling, and duality — with zero laser-safety worries.
